Human and Subhuman in M and Rome Open City

Jonathan Kocevar October 13, 2021 Reading Time: approx. 4 min.

    The 1930s to the 1940s were decades marked by the rise of authoritarianism, gruesome warfare, and horrific atrocities, and films of the era mirrored the same terrible acts into their narratives using overarching themes. One important theme often displayed is the idea of “human versus sub-human”. This theme is heavily used in both the German expressionist film M and the Italian neorealist film Rome Open City. Both films were produced during both the beginning and crumble of the regimes of the Third Reich and Fascist Italy, and both reflect the deeply ingrained social attitudes on race and the human/sub-human divisions held during the time, trying to tackle the racial dogma and encapsulate it inside the films.

    M and Rome Open City both heavily use the theme of the “sub-human” in “The Trial” and “The Salon” scenes respectively, with much of the set and narrative highlighting the theme to the audience. For example, in “The Trial” scene from M, the Mise-en-scène was set perfectly to emphasize the ostracization of the “sub-human” Hans Beckert from the group. Beckert is placed alone on his side of the set and is also placed physically lower than the judges and the jury, having to look up while on his knees and beg to the crowd. The film has no other sounds other than the argument within the kangaroo court, with the lone Beckert pleading from one side, and the unitary masses on the other chanting for his death, again pointing out the theme of the ostracized sub-human versus the group/humans. The scene “The Salon” in Rome Open City also portrays the theme of the “sub-human” from a different perspective, with the social rejection of the master-slave racial logic that plagued the decades before the making of the film within Fascist Italy and Nazi Germany. The scene is set within a salon filled with German high command, sharply dressed, surrounded by classical art and decor. The dialogue focuses on the argument about the German “master-race” between Major Bergmann and a cynical Major Hartmann. Major Hartmann rejects the racial arguments of Bergmann asserting that the subjugation of the people perceived to be “sub-humans” by Bergmann only leads to “unquenchable hatred” and that the Germans will die “without the slightest hope.” The diegetic piano music within the scene, combined with the renaissance decor of the set also reinforces the idea of high culture and civilization, many perceived characteristics of what the National Socialists and Fascists believed were parts of the “master-race” over “sub-humans”.

    Professor of Italian film studies and literature Peter Bondanella presents another take on the dialogue between Major Hartmann and Major Bergmann, stating that he believes that director Rosselini uses the dialogue to antagonize and critique the German attitude during the Nazi regime, Where Hartmann is portrayed as the first conscious German in the film who rejects the Nazi race science, dismissing Bergmann’s arguments. I personally agree with this opinion, as the context of the times in which the movie was made is during the end of the crumbling social dogma and false-race science that was engrained in both Italian and German society, and the antagonization of the occupying Germans by the Italians during the end of the war.

    While both M and Rome Open City seem very different in their portrayals of “superior/master humans over sub-humans”, both can also be interpreted with many connections and similarities. Both films highlight the rejection of master/slave, human/sub-human thought, and present the theme that people are equal and can’t be judged by isolated characteristics. In the trial scene in M, this argument takes the form of Beckert’s assigned defense counsel arguing that Schranker, the very judge accusing Beckert of being a crazed maniac who must be killed, was guilty of manslaughter himself, pointing out the contradictions of the human and superior judge and jury over sub-human criminal Beckert, asserting that they are not as different as they think. German poet and writer Horst Lange write about the convictions of Schranker during the trial scene in M, believing it not to be an argument from the standpoint of business and return to normalcy, but an emotionally charged argument, dividing himself from Beckert. Schranker is looking for a way to distance himself using emotional rhetoric, and creating a line between what acts are human and what acts are sub-human. This same theme of the portrayed subhuman as equal to the master-race is echoed within Rome Open City, with Major Hartmann stating that the enemies of Germany and their portrayed master-race were just as fierce and worthy as the Germans, and all their conquest in the name of superiority had gotten them was more enemies and casualties. Both films clearly portray the idea of equality between humans and races and reject the master-slave dichotomy of their time.

    In conclusion, Both the films M and Rome Open City tackle one of the most important social conflicts of their time and use the powerful theme of humans versus sub-humans to portray the collective attitudes of society during the rise and fall of Fascism and National Socialism on film.
