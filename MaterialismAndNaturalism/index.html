Materialism and Naturalism: The Harmony of Marx and Aristotle

Jonathan Kocevar January 6, 2020 Reading Time: approx. 10 min.

    Karl Marx and Aristotle were both realists and materialists, science being held to the highest degree when forming their theories and philosophies. Both were born into chaotic and shifting societies during times of crisis and found philosophy as a way to explain and solve practical problems that faced them every day. which would shape their ideas, whether it is the constantly battling city-states or the rise of industrial capitalism in Western Europe. Through the analysis of both philosopher’s thoughts on ethics and alienation, metaphysics and view on religion, it is clear that the ideas of Karl Marx and Aristotle hold many similarities and share many ideas, even with being separated by over 2000 years.

    The historical and local backgrounds of both of the philosophers share extremely similar environments that had affected the light in which they thought and formulated their theories. Karl Marx was born in the Prussian city of Trier during 1818, a rapid time of change in Europe after the fall of Napoleon a short three years earlier. Napoleon’s campaigns had left Europe in scrambles rebuilding itself and the ideological embers created by the French Revolution waiting to be sparked once more. Marx’s family was very deeply rooted in those embers— his father being a heavy supporter of the idealism of men like Kant and Voltaire and the ideas of the enlightenment, which would carry into Marx’s formative years and change many of his fundamental views. Marx’s early life aligned with the beginning of the major powers of Europe developing the means of labor, such as more advanced machinery and tools, a time period known as the Industrial Revolution, and the major focus of his works and ideology. Marx saw firsthand the shift from artisan crafting of singular items to mass, factory-scale commodity production owned by the rich or bourgeois, which he saw as a major problem for the new poor industrial working class, called the proletariat. Marx had formulated that the proletariat was slowly becoming isolated from its labor and work and being controlled by any means, whether it be organized religion, the law, or bourgeois government propaganda. Nearly 1500 years before Marx, the ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle was born in 384 BC in the northern city-state of Stagira in Greece. Much like the political culture and geopolitics of Marx’s formative years, Greece had been torn and left destroyed in the Peloponnesian war only 20 years before his birth, which would go on to be conquered by the Macedonians and forced to accept the non-homogenous rule of foreigners and their conflicting traditions and concepts. Not only did the occupation bring the cultures and radical ideas of the northern Macedonians to Greece, causing conflict in the marketplace of ideas, but also created a time of peace which led to great philosophical and political growth, allowing the young Aristotle to enroll at Plato’s Academy and join the philosophical practice within Greece until 347 BC, the year of Plato’s death. In 335, Aristotle would create his own academy labeled the Lyceum, where he would focus on a wide range of subjects, especially subjects such as the history of philosophy, metaphysics, psychology, theology, and political philosophy, the same subjects that Marx would grasp and try to solve during his time.

    Both Marx and Aristotle had extremely similar views on the matter of both organized religion and the application of faith towards a society. Aristotle was a very staunch critic of organized religion throughout his life, being a naturalist first and foremost, the study and use of god were practical rather than faithful in his worldview. Aristotle also critiqued the way religion was used practically by the leading figures of his time, such as politicians and military leaders, as a way to coerce the population into following their orders. In his book Politics, Aristotle writes:

“A tyrant must put on the appearance of uncommon devotion to religion. Subjects are less apprehensive of illegal treatment from a ruler whom they consider god-fearing and pious. On the other hand, they do less easily move against him, believing that he has the gods on his side.”1

    Aristotle is stating that who he believed to be the strong tyrant rulers of his time used religion as a tool against political opponents and as a way to pacify the average citizen. Even if the tyrant was not believing or faithful, Aristotle saw organized religion as a major tool authority could use to push individuals to act a certain way. Marx would have agreed entirely with Aristotle’s analysis of Organized Religion, even further expanding on it in his own writings. In his work A Contribution to the Critique of Hegel’s Philosophy of the Right, Marx infamously proclaims “Religion is the Opiate of the People”. While often misunderstood and misquoted, Marx was directly building upon Aristotle’s critique of organized religion as nothing more than a controller of the masses. Rosie Blau of the Economist perfectly encapsulates Marx’s view on religion and his well-known quote:

“Marx was not exactly against religion. For him, faith was something that “the people” conjured for themselves, a source of phony happiness to which they turned to help numb the pain of reality. It was “the sigh of the oppressed creature”. Organized religion with its churches, doctrines and priests followed on from that, a useful tool by which the ruling classes kept the masses supine.”2

Both Marx and Aristotle held very cynical and critical views upon organized religion, but both also had understood the power of local, horizontally structured, non-hierarchical religion. In Marx’s work The Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, he writes, “To develop in greater spiritual freedom, a people must break their bondage to their bodily needs—they must cease to be the slaves of the body. They must, above all, have time at their disposal for spiritual creative activity and spiritual enjoyment.”3 Marx saw communal religion and spirituality as an aid in an individual’s freedom and dissociation of past bourgeois institutions and saw it as an improvement tenfold compared to the organized religion and church apparatuses of his day. Aristotle, while being the critic of organized religion as nothing more than a powerbase for tyrants, like Marx held that religion and spirituality itself was not a bad thing. In the centuries following his death and the rise of Christianity, Aristotle’s metaphysics and ethics would go on the form the framework for the basis of Christian theology and ontology.4 In Aristotle’s ethics book Nicomachean Ethics, the words ‘god’ and ‘divine’ occur roughly twice as often as the words ‘happiness’ and ‘happy’, it directly equivocates that a form of god is at the very center of Aristotle’s ethical philosophy and that happiness is gained from it, again implying the innate goodness of non-organized religion.5 Both Karl Marx and Aristotle’s views and philosophies on religion were highly critical of the institution of religion, and both agreed on the fact of communal religion and spirituality being an emancipator of the individual overall.

    Both philosophers shared many of the same philosophical concepts in their metaphysical theories, Marx’s being his philosophy of science, and Aristotle’s being his Natural Realism. In direct contrast to his mentor and teacher Plato, Aristotle turned from the idealist worldview of the Academy and began creating his own metaphysical framework of the world, which would take the form of Natural Realism. To Aristotle, the immaterial was not of direct use when understanding the world around him. He was against the use of the theory of forms as an explanation to the world and began to look directly in the physical world around him. Aristotle would use this realist worldview to explain many phenomena through science, by using the scientific formula.6 In direct contrast to his recent philosopher predecessors such as Kant, Marx began his metaphysical theory based on the reading of Hegel and interpolation of Hegel’s theory of dialectics. Marx would go on to make his theory of science within Marxism and the dialectic to form the concrete groundwork for his materialist claims. Early socialist and communist predecessors to Marx, such as the ones sprouting from the French Revolution, had been extremely utopian and had not made any significant progress on staying realistic and grounded within the economic and technological concepts within their own time.7 Marx had argued that communism and socialism were more than a utopian ideal and that using dialectic and natural science to draw a path for the understanding of communist society would be both more practical, and more important to the movement as a whole.7 In his book The Communist Manifesto, Marx begins to explain his use of realism and materialism in his work and theory of communism. Marx writes, “The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles,” using his dialectic, Marx is able to uncover the materialist approach to history. Marx had applied a scientific approach to the history before him, rather than seeing it as idealistic and being the stories of the past of “great men” and “heroic struggle”, Marx had seen a formula arise of classes overthrowing each other and becoming dominant, which he then formed the hypothesis for Communism out of. Marx’s materialist history is very similar to the natural realist use of the scientific method to prove premises, which Karl had done to explain the class struggles as a wider net over world history than previously imagined, creating the basis for Communist thought.

    One of the most important connections both Karl Marx and Aristotle shared was the idea of Entfremdung (Alienation), Eudaemonia (Happiness/Welfare), and their shared ideas of the individual. Aristotle had believed the word of Eudaimonia, the idea in Greek society of ultimate happiness and a healthy and flourishing man, which is gained through virtue and excellence of the singular man. In Nicomadian Ethics, Aristotle writes:

“The happy life is thought to be one of excellence; now an excellent life requires exertion, and does not consist in amusement … If Eudaimonia, or happiness, is an activity in accordance with excellence, it is reasonable that it should be in accordance with the highest excellence; and this will be that of the best thing in us.”8

Aristotle was arguing that real happiness comes from effort, a strive and desire for completion. Excellence to Aristotle holds the Greek ideal of aretê, the focus on virtue and hard work. He is stating that the singular individual needs to specify what region of work they excel in over the others, to gain the largest amount of Eudaemonia, or happiness. Aristotle believed that eudemonia was not only good for society, but it was also good for the singular individual, who would strive at an occupation they were genuinely good at. This idea of “let the individual do what corresponds the best to their skills” was very important during his time and for many centuries later, where the major form of production above the slave and serf class was through labor guilds of small groups, and artisans, who singularly crafted handmade goods. Marx was egalitarian and would have aligned with Aristotle on the point of Eudaimonia, and that it is ideal to strive for complete happiness of society and happiness of the individual. Marx believed that the capitalist mode of production did not allow for one to be able to reach Eudaimonia or fulfillment, and believed that Alienation of labor was the problem. In his Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts of 1844, Marx states:

“What, then, constitutes the alienation of labor? First, in the fact that labor is external to the worker, that is, that it does not belong to his essential being; that in his work, therefore, he does not affirm himself but denies himself, does not feel well but unhappy, does not freely develop his physical and mental energy but mortifies his body and ruins his mind. The worker, therefore, feels himself only outside his work and feels beside himself in his work. He is at home when he is not working, and when he is working he is not at home. His work, therefore, is not voluntary, but coerced; it is forced labor. It is therefore not the satisfaction of a need, but only a means for satisfying needs external to it. Its alien character emerges clearly in the fact that labor is shunned like the plague as soon as there is no physical or another compulsion.”9

Marx is explaining how his theory of labor alienation breaks both his and Aristotle’s view of the ideal excellence that every human can strive to become. Marx believed that as a worker is exploited by the bourgeois, forced to work shift labor, they will never experience the true passion and excellence that comes with being a free individual/artisan. Aristotle throughout his book Nicomachean Ethics points out the idea of the individual putting to use their talents as a strength to join both work and fulfillment to be one of the foremost goals of every human. Marx argues alienation of the worker, forcing the individual to do menial tasks and only represent a fraction of the production (eg. Making only the seats of bikes, leaving the rest of the labor to be shared amongst their coworkers at the factory) doomed the capitalist mode of production and earlier variants to ever achieve Eudaemonia. It is apparent that Karl Marx’s theory of labor alienation and Aristotle’s virtue of Eudaemonia are both closely linked and share very similar concepts.

    To Summarize, Philosophers Karl Marx and Aristotle clearly both share many of the same ideas and were very similar in their beliefs. This is clear by analyzing the political and local history of both of the philosophers, their agreeance on organized religion and spirituality, the similarities between Marx’s Philosophy of science and Aristotle’s natural realism, and comparing the likeness between the ideas of Entfremdung and Eudaemonia. Both Karl Marx and Aristotle will be remembered in time as part of the greatest thinkers for generations to come, and whether it is political, economic, ethical, or metaphysical—their ideas will be engrained in history as monumental leaps in philosophy and mankind
