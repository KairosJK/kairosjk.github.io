The Psychology of Radicalization

Jonathan Kocevar April 3, 2022 Reading Time: approx. 5 min.

    “The personal is political”, this infamous 60’s activist slogan could not be more correct when talking about radicalization. In the modern world every action is political; whether it be choosing what show to watch on TV or what to buy in a store. Making choices that incentivize one group over others, politics is the act of choosing how to enforce one’s interests. Politics dictate how our world acts and forms the basis for society. The contemporary political landscape has led to many marginalized groups feeling disenfranchised and gaining anti-establishment ideas and concepts. This is the basis of radicalization. The driving force for most political movements of the 19th and 20th centuries has been radicalization, be it the Bolsheviks or the Taliban. Understanding one’s motives and conditions of radicalization can aid professionals to halt violent radicalization or to understand the mechanisms which make one become radicalized and stabilize the conditions. With the recent rise of Islamic terrorist acts in the West and the re-emergence of white nationalism with far-right, domestic terrorist incidents, understanding and thwarting the threat of radicalization is extremely important.

    The field of political psychology and radicalism is a relatively new discipline that is beginning to grow in response to the rise of violent terror attacks and the attempt to halt them. One of the leading psychologists in the field of radicalization and terrorism is Robert Barret, a professional working with Nigerian terrorist cells. He follows active members and documents their actions and mindsets. Barret theorizes that there are six motivational forces that drive one to become politically radicalized; “the follower, the pragmatist, the criminal, the soldier, the basic needs, and the idealogue”.1 Barret uses these forces to underline basic methods and paths of extremism; examples include endorsement of ethnic supremacy as being “idealogue”, freedom to commit crime as the “criminal”, and supposed injustice within the system as the “soldier”. Barrett also researched the recruiting drives from radical groups and found that radicalized independants believed they were volunteering, with recruiters making “coercion feel like volunteerism”.1 John Horgan, a Pennsylvania State University psychologist, has stated that radicalization is not cause-effect or pathologic, rather that there is traits found that are advertently linked to radicalization; “the feeling of disenfranchisement, cynical and pessimistic of political involvement, identifying with victims of social injustice, believing that engaging violence against the state is not immoral, and joining a cell or movement heightened sense of identity”.2 The important point that Horgan states is that the paths are not cause and effect, individuals can associate with traits of radicalization, but not be radical, which makes the topic of extremism in the psychological community less about why does someone become radicalized and rather how does society stop or minimize the risk factors that lead to paths of extremism and radicalization.

    The identifiers and signals hypothesised by psychologists like John Horgan and Robert Barret allow for specialists to try to thwart violent attacks and halt radicalization of groups. Understanding the psychology of what causes one to join a radical group or terrorist organization and stopping the paths that allow one to become radicalized is more effective than suppression and direct contact with the group. In the late eighties, a Peruvian Maoist insurgency group named “Shining Path” occupied many districts of Peru and was a major political force inside the country, with thousands of members and high ranking officials working in the school districts interacting with the youth.3 In the late 1990’s, the Peruvian government used psychology to solve the problem by cutting off the recruitment paths that the radicalized used to indoctrinate and influence others, raiding the school boards, infiltrating neighborhoods, and seeking and isolating the Maoists. Once disconnected from all sources of recruitment, and with high officials in prison, the Shining Path declined immensely.4 Another government combating radicalization is in Aarhus, Denmark. The “Aarhus Model” seeks to stop young people from joining Islamic extremist groups such as ISIS or Al-Qaeda by giving counselling and monitoring services for the radicalized.5 By giving the potentially radicalized support and understanding their mindset, the individuals are able to integrate into society better than enforcing strict crackdown policies. The radicalized gain support and form a bond of trust between authority and the individual, instead of the individual antagonizing authority.

    The future of the study of political psychology and radicalization is the only way of permanently lowering the population of radicalized people. Suppression of thought, denial of solving the roots of radicalization, oppression and physical force only delays insurgent efforts, but ensures that the force will become more omnipresent.6 The study of political psychology intends to do the opposite, realizing the only way to effectively stop insurgency is by cutting down the communities radicals influence and recruit from. In Louise Richardson’s book, “What Terrorists Want: Understanding the Enemy, Containing the Threat”, Richardson argues that to undermine the threat of radicalization and terrorism, the governing body must “Remove the Terrorists from their Communities”. Richardson states that with the knowledge of what causes a person to become radicalized, or rather the modifiers which allow conversion to become easier, that cause should be removed from the community to lower the force multiplier of one becoming radicalized, subsequently lowering the percentage of an individual becoming radicalized from their community. Richardson’s’s concept of isolation of terrorists from communities could be by the presence of the United States Military in Afghanistan and Iraq. Since the United States Military entrance into the Middle East, US officials have failed to stop the terrorist and radical cells from indoctrinating and recruiting communities with “us” vs “them” sentiments. The study of radicalization is the embodiment of the behaviour of humans. As aforementioned, politics is the enforcement of one’s ideals; the struggle of enforcing ideals that counter the existing ideas implemented in society and engrained in institutions and is the driving force of political strife and radicalization. In a society where every action is political, select members of that society will feel marginalized by the society’s actions or ideas. Radicalization shows how the interaction of those marginalized individuals choose to combat the established order, groups form for what each member of that society deems is the best for their situation, whether that be a political party or a far-right or left vanguard insurgency to see their ideals enforced.

    In conclusion, the developments of political psychology and radicalization are crucial to stopping violent attacks globally and halting the formation of insurgent groups that are a threat to safety. It is clear that the theories psychologists have implemented in the past were successful, showing that learning the paths and methods of extremism can allow permanent solutions to the problems of radicalization and are the clearest way of both helping the marginalized and curbing the threat of extremism in the future.
