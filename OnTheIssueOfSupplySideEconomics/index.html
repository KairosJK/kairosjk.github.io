On the Issue of Supply-Side Economics

Jonathan Kocevar January 21, 2020 Reading Time: approx. 11 min.

Introduction

    “The rich get richer and the poor get poorer”, an infamous quote by Percey Shelly paints an exemplary picture of one of the major discourses in the modern macroeconomic debate, the support of Supply-side Economics. The theory was sparked from the question of what the most effective theory and policy was to implement in a country to stimulate growth. The theory essentially argues that market regulation and taxation should be rescinded to allow for the private sector to develop the market instead of the government. The theory of Supply-side Economics is an ineffective way of stimulating economic growth and historically has caused many problems where it has been tried. Through the analysis of its origin and history, negative application in modern economics and criticizing the proponents of Supply-side Economics, it is clear that Supply-side Economics is not the fit macroeconomic policy for the modern political landscape.

The History and Origin of the Supply-Side Theory

    The Macroeconomic Theory of Economics has its roots during the stagflation and recession of the mid-1970s, carrying all the way into the early 1980s.1 The economic woes were dealt from the slowly receding economic development and growth of the post-World War II eras. This issue combined with the oil crises that hit America in the 1970s, left the American Government to turn to radically new macroeconomic ideas and different approaches to the relationship between the government and domestic businesses. In the early 1980s, economist and member of President Reagan’s Economic Policy Advisory Board, Arthur Laffer began to theorize the core arguments for a new economic theory which would later be called Supply-side Economics. The main theory being that overall economic growth can most effectively be encouraged through the act of lowering commercial taxes and decreasing regulation within the industrial and commercial sectors.

    The core idea was that this would begin to solve the economic problems that had been facing the United States during the recession and hit President Ford’s and President Reagan’s economic goals, such as price stability, full employment and the expansion of economic freedom. This was a very radical difference compared to the macroeconomic systems of the 1960s and earlier, where most governments took a Keynesian, Demand-Side policy, focusing on the stimulation of the economy through monetary stimulus and heavy government involvement in the markets. (examples including the New Deal of the 1930s, and the Postwar Marshall Plan). Today, the argument of Supply-side vs Demand-Side economics is still a point of major contention. Crises such as the 2007-2008 recession and the 2020 Covid-19 pandemic have been important forums and topics of debate for economists on the most adaptable and stable macroeconomic policy and has highlighted many problems in the Supply-side Policy, such as needed stimulus for businesses to stay afloat and tax funding to revitalizing the public and private sector.

Arguments against Supply-Side Economics

    First, the social equality and general economical aspects of supply-side economics and Reaganomics are flawed and deeply rooted in incorrect premises. Reaganomics, and the idea of trickle-down economics, is one core idea of the Supply-Side theory. Trickle-down economics supposes that the government should loosen tax laws on businesses and the wealthy within the country, as a way to encourage stimulus in the form of reinvestment and through the funding of works by investors to in turn help the public and meeting economic goals proposed by the government through private investors instead of direct government spending. The idea is still widely seen as a good theory to solving economic problems within conservative circles and had been used extensively during Trump’s term as the President of the United States.2 The idea of trickle-down economics encouraging development has been widely researched throughout the 1980s, and 1990s, with some researchers studying its effects up to the present day.34 Economist David Hope of the London School of Economics and Julian Limberg of King’s College, London had studied the policy of trickle-down economics from the 1970s to 2015, and wrote in their paper:

“We find that major reforms reducing taxes on the rich lead to higher income inequality as measured by the top 1% share of pre-tax national income. The effect remains stable in the medium term. In contrast, such reforms do not have any significant effect on economic growth and unemployment.”5

The paper states that no correlation exists between private investment in the public sector and aid to the poor. It essentially states that the tax money that the government released back to the wealthy 1%, had not gone to investment but rather to other means for the rich to accumulate even more of the wealth in the economy. A study from the Tax Justice Network (TJN) shows that the tax cuts to the rich did not go to rebuilding and reinvesting into the economy, but rather to be accumulated in overseas tax havens and tax shelters, serving no use to the domestic economy. The leader of the research at the TJN, John Christensen said about the issue:

“Inequality is much, much worse than official statistics show, but politicians are still relying on trickle-down to transfer wealth to poorer people … This new data shows the exact opposite has happened: for three decades extraordinary wealth has been cascading into the offshore accounts of a tiny number of super-rich.”

The effects of this wealth hoarding have been extremely harmful and detrimental towards the middle and lower working class of not only America but every developed nation’s economy that had been affected by the theory of trickle-down economics. Research conducted by the Economic Policy Institute summated in a graph shows the overwhelming wealth disparity.

(For Image Credit, See6)

The research shows that the top 1% (the red), and the Bottom 90% (the light blue) have progressed from around the same amount of wealth shared (~35%) before Reagan’s term, where progressive tax rates implemented by previous administrations were still present (near a 70% marginal rate). When Reagan began his term in 1981, his tax breaks implemented only a year into his first term, lowering from the 70% top bracket marginal tax rate to 50% marginal tax rate.7 The Pre-Reagan rates have never gone back to since, which has allowed the rich to accumulate and snowball their wealth. By the end of Reagan’s Presidency, the national tax rate on top-bracket earners would be 28%, a whopping 60% decrease.7 Not only have the ideals of Supply-side Economics failed to meet their proposed economic goals, such as price stability from the rising inflation of the 70s, full employment, and the expansion of economic freedom to corporations but have also deepened other economic problems such as the negative Equitable Distribution of Income. The arguments for the support of Supply-side Economics and support of Trickle-down Economics is factually unfounded.

    Second, another problem with the theory of Supply-side Economics is that one of its sole purposes, encouraging economic goals through the use of the private sector, does not happen. Productivity being the first major goal, the idea that lowering commercial and top bracket tax rates will allow business owners and investors to reinvest into better technology to improve productivity and ultimately push the production possibility curve to the right. Investment growth being the second goal, the idea that the tax rescinded dollars would go towards reinvestment in new firms or into the expansion of firms. Both these goals were stunted when the Supply-side policy was introduced, leading to inefficiency and lagging of the domestic economy.

(For Image Credit, See8)

Both productivity and reinvestment being major important aspects to the theory, it is unexpected to see the following results. Both worker productivity and investment growth had been less in supply side economies, versus Demand-side economies, which had a better effect in both categories. Investment growth had more than a 9% increase in the demand-side model, even when accounting for the 1990s recession.8 Two of Supply-sides major premises had been impeded on and limited by the economic policies of the supply-side model.

    Third, the issue of Supply side Economics did not only affect the efficiency of industry and the commercial sector, but also affected the government’s ability to spend and was attributed largely to the beginning of the increase of debt that would soon later skyrocket. The annotated graph below shows this exact increase in debt.

It can be argued that public debt rise itself is not a bad thing necessarily, as long as the government has enough to pay it off as their capital markets expand with them, but under Reagan and his economic policy the debt held as a percentage of the GDP increased to over 30%. That is not only the public debt, but it is compared to the GDP and the capital markets available to the United States Government. The result of this was an uninterrupted ride increasing the public debt that the US continues to see today (other than a short term under Clinton). The tax cuts and deregulation that Reagan had enforced in 1981-82 did in reality not only do relatively nothing to kickstart the economy, but actually damaged the economy by adding large deficits to the federal budget to spend more on defense by spending money they no longer had10, but also removed many small business funding programs and grants that had allowed the businesses to thrive.11 It is perspicuous that Reagan’s Supply-side model had not only hurt the economy, but also the federal budget and debt as well.

The Opposition, The Arguments for Supply-Side Economics

    One of the major arguments for Supply-side Economics is its historical use during Reagan’s presidency and its sentiment among supporters as a major factor in fixing the recessions of the 1970s and helping supercharge the economy in the following years. Many supporters of Reaganomics policies will turn toward the 1980s and look at the correlation between Reagan’s tax cuts for the top bracket and the Real GDP of the United States to explain the economic revitalization of the US during the 80s12 such as the ones below.

While it is true that an economic rebound did take place during Reagan’s terms, the reasons for the rebound are deeper than his Supply-side policies. As learned in class, interest rates are one of the most important tools and stimulators of the economy that the government has available. Many attribute the growth of the real GDP to Reagan’s tax cuts and in turn the economic stimulation of the economy by the wealthy business owners and investors when in reality, this had little effect. The larger effect was the Federal Reserve’s responses to interest rates during the early 1980s. In 1981, the Federal Reserve bumped interest rates up to almost 20%, to facilitate low spending and risk in the economy, halting it to fix the inflation of the 1970s, as money will not circulate if there is too much risk to invest in the market. This led to a larger recession, which the rebound that would hit the next year is the recession Reagan is usually accredited to by supporters of Supply-side theory. The more probable cause is that when the Federal Reserve would lower the rates back to 13% in 1982, confidence was gained again by investors and banks to begin trading and lending money again. The annotated graph below again helps explain how the Real GDP growth correlates with the Federal Reserve raising of the interest rates.

(For Image Credit, See15)

The recession of 1981 can be attributed to the Federal Reserves raising interest rates, and many economic historians agree on the fact that the market upturn was majorly in response to the lowering of the interest rates again causing the markets to rescind from isolation and begin trading and dealmaking again. This Real GDP rise in 1982 also compounded with the decrease of inflation caused by the Federal Reserve raising interest rates.16 It is clear that the arguments supporting the use of Supply-side Economics through argumentation of the history of the theory and its implementation in Reagan’s economic policy and US markets upturn are unreasonable and unfactual.

Relation to the Local Economy

    The issue of Supply-side economics is deeply rooted within Ontario, with one of the most adamant of supporters and theorizers for Supply-side economics being Kingston-born Canadian Economist Robert Mundell. Many of the policies taken into account during Reagan’s and some of Kennedy’s administrations were directly modeled on what Mundell believed should have happened to the East and West of Canada, especially in the Southern Ontario car sector and the Northern Ontario lumber sector. These industries crossed borders a numerous amount of times throughout Canada and America and Mundell saw the tax rates and government regulations as a damage to the fluidity of the trades between the two states.17 The proposals of using supply-side policy when it comes to the border is a double edge sword. While it does reduce costs in taxation and reduces prerequisites to ship cross country, it also brings along easy crime and disease, and also forces many domestically made products out of the country and forces more imports in turn, allowing for American domination of the Canadian economy. Toronto has also had one of the largest problems with the ideals of Supply-side policies within the city. The housing market of the city has been on the rise for years and shows no sign of slowing down. The chart below shows that since 2012, real estate value in Toronto has nearly doubled. The reason for this is the lack of government regulation and intervention in the process.

(For Image Credit, See18)

The regional municipality of Toronto had pushed a motion to require at least three years’ supply of serviced or ready land for a range of different residential buildings.19 Today, the metropolitan government follows these rules loosely if not at all and it has led the Toronto housing market to spiral.20 The creation of new single-detached family homes has gone from 30,000 units during 2001, to 15.000 units in 2014, leading to a large demand in one-family residential plots.20 This problem of non-government intervention is not only seen in the single-family residential sector but also in the condominium and apartment markets. This loose government grasp on plot allocation combined with the low-interest rates have left for large demand on leasing and buying a property with enticing market possibilities within Toronto, but the libertarian Supply-side attitude by the policymakers have left Toronto’s economic possibilities short.21 It is clear that even on the local scale, Supply-side Economics sells short the opportunity and market prospects of the Ontario area.

Conclusion

    To summarize, through analysis and argumentation of the origins and background of Supply-side economics, criticism of the proponents of Supply-side theory and examination of effects on the local economy, it is implicit that Supply-side Economics is an ineffective and harmful macroeconomic strategy when applied to the modern real world. Social satirist and journalist H.L. Mencken once wrote, “For every complex problem there is an answer that is clear, simple, and wrong.” It perfectly encapsulates the macroeconomic theory of Supply-side policy and its harmful effects on the complex and captivating study that is contemporary economics and that it is not fit for the modern political and economic landscape of the 21st century.
